People Helping People


Spring, 2025
Group Project




Project Description:


People Helping People was a website design project assigned during the spring semester, 2025. This project hypothetically served as a way to better connect people who would need help, such as the elderly or disabled, with volunteers. The problem proposed in the beginning was the imaginary client, a non-profit community foundation named People Helping People, relied on phone calls to connect users and spreadsheet. Us as the designers were assigned to streamline the process with the user in mind. The solutions proposed was a matching system which includes user profiles and filling out forms to connect users with each other.




Design Process:


1. Personas


To start, the potential target users were considered. Three personas were created to represent possible prominent users: disabled, elderly and volunteers.



2. Wireframing


After taking the users into account, designing the website came next. Aspects of the website were created through wireframing with Figma such as:


  • Form and profile pages
  • Possible layouts
  • Typography and colors


3. Prototype


Lastly, I along with a small team built the website using the wireframes prior. This was done through standard HTML and CSS. My contribution on the project was the design and implementation of the home and profile page for the website.

Final Prototype:

Challenges & What I've Learned


A difficulty of the project for me was figuring out how the system would theoretically work and what elements should be included for the user experience. Ultimately, we decided to keep it simple. The users would be able to request assistance as well as offer their services for anyone who may need it.


I learned many things throughout this project. Not only did I learn about the design process, I also learned visual design principles. Such examples include layouts, typography and colors, and the importance of these principles to serve the user. Practicing these concepts throughout the semester culminated in the final prototype deliverable.


The design went through many ideas and iterations. Some concepts were not included in the final prototype. For example, some additional wireframes I worked on were:


  • Grid layout to see and browse other users
  • Different home and profile user interface layouts
  • Mobile interface wireframe

These designs did not make the final prototype due to it being out of scope, overly complicated or visually unappealing.

League of Legends Feature


Fall, 2024
Solo Project




Project Description:


This project is a design project that hoped to incorporate third party websites' features into the League of Legends client itself. The problem I tried to solve with this was that information on in-game characters differed depending on different websites. My solution was to have these features within the client so users could have access to more accurate numbers. In addition, players could also gain a better understanding of the champions in terms of game-play. These solutions include features such as character win-rates, counter-picks and player-created guides for champions.




Design Process:


1. Lean UX Canvas


The Lean UX Canvas by Jeff Gothelf was used to create the final deliverable. The steps I took were:


  • Business Problem - 3rd party website inaccuracy
  • Business Outcomes - More player engagement and activity
  • Target Users - League of Legends players
  • User Benefits - Game knowledge, optimization and learning
  • Solution Ideas - Player guides, character statistics


2. Wireframing


The final deliverable is a wireframe of the features I wanted to bring to the League of Legends client. I was the sole designer as this was a solo project. Additionally, this deliverable was created through Figma.

UWSP Parking App


Fall, 2023
Group Project




Project Description:


This is a design project that aimed to let UW Stevens Point students find more accessible parking anywhere on campus. The problem was that students often have trouble during the parking process such as buying permits or just searching for good parking. The solutions we hypothesized was to create a mobile app to streamline the process. Some features of the app would be map functionality, browsing and selection of parking spots, in-app transactions.




Design Process:


1. Analysis


First, we analyzed the problem and discovered the pain points of the current system. The permit acquisition process is inconvenient. Students are placed on a wait list when trying to get a permit and are only allowed in one parking lot.



2. Solution Ideas


An idea we developed was a user would be able to use the app to see what parking spots on campus is available. Then, the user could book a specific parking spot for a period of time. Additionally, they would also pay an immediate fee for the reservation. Students could still be able to purchase parking permits and use them as another option.



3. User Scenario


Using our solution ideas, we came up with a user scenario to see how a typical user would interact with our hypothetical app.



4. Wireframe Mockup


Lastly, we created a wireframe mockup of how the app would look like. My role on the project was to create these mockups and I did so with paper and pencil.




Challenges & What I learned


Initially, we were unsure of what would be appropriate for an app such as this. We ended up overthinking and designing beyond the scope of the project. Ideas we came up with were outside the app and were not relevant such as physical sensors at parking locations. To scale it back, we focused on just the app itself and what features it may have to serve the users.


Having worked on this, it taught me to redirect my focus even on a group project such as this. Similar to writing a paper, it is easy to get sidetracked. The best way to combat this is to take a step back and see if what was produced answers the problem or question.
